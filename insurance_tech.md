# Insurance Technology Regulation Reference

> **Scope:** Federal and NAIC model law framework governing insurance technology, agency operations, MGA structures, and digital distribution.  
> **Source Note:** Primary source (NAIC) is Tier 2.5 — quasi-governmental. NAIC model laws are not binding until adopted by individual states. State-specific activation required for binding state rules.  
> **Weight:** Moderate velocity at federal level. High velocity at state level as states adopt NAIC model bulletins on AI and digital distribution.  
> **Reminder:** This is a reference, not legal advice. Get qualified review before commercial launch.

---

## Federal Framework

### No Federal Insurance Regulator (McCarran-Ferguson Act)
Insurance regulation is primarily state-based by federal statute. The federal role is limited to:
- Anti-trust oversight (limited)
- Federal programs (flood, crop, terrorism insurance)
- Interstate commerce issues

**Practical implication:** Most binding rules come from state DOIs. Core registry covers federal and NAIC model laws. State activation required for binding state rules.

### Bank Secrecy Act / Anti-Money Laundering (AML)
Insurance companies and some agents have AML obligations depending on products sold. Life insurance with investment components (variable products) have the most significant AML requirements.

**Applies if:** Selling life insurance, annuities, or variable products.

### USA PATRIOT Act — Customer Identification
Certain insurance entities must maintain customer identification programs (CIP) for AML purposes.

## NAIC Model Laws and Bulletins (Tier 2.5)

> These are model frameworks. Binding only in states that have adopted them. Watch state activation for jurisdiction-specific status.

### NAIC AI Model Bulletin (2023)
Provides guidance on insurer use of AI/ML in underwriting, pricing, and claims. Key provisions:
- Insurers responsible for AI outcomes regardless of whether developed internally or by a vendor
- Must be able to explain decisions made by AI/ML models
- Prohibition on proxy discrimination — using variables that are proxies for protected characteristics
- Documentation requirements for AI model governance
- States actively adopting versions: CO, CT, IL, MN are leaders

**Status:** Rapidly being adopted. Treat as likely binding within 2–3 years in most states.

### NAIC Producer Licensing Model Act
Governs licensing requirements for insurance producers (agents/brokers) across states. Key points:
- Resident and non-resident licensing requirements
- Continuing education obligations
- Appointment and termination requirements with carriers
- Background check and disclosure obligations

### NAIC Market Conduct Model
Standards for how insurance is marketed and sold. Relevant to digital distribution:
- Advertisement standards apply to digital/online marketing
- Disclosure requirements for online quoting and binding
- E-commerce and electronic records compliance (UETA/E-SIGN Act)

### NAIC Privacy of Consumer Financial and Health Information (Model Reg 672)
Mirrors GLBA requirements for insurance. Requires privacy notices and opt-out for data sharing. States widely adopted.

### NAIC Cybersecurity Model Law (Model 668)
Requires insurers (and entities licensed by state DOIs) to maintain an information security program. Includes:
- Written information security program
- Risk assessment
- Incident response plan
- Annual certification requirement (for larger entities)
- Third-party service provider oversight

**Status:** Adopted by most states. Treat as broadly binding.

## MGA / Agency-Specific Rules

### Managing General Agent (MGA) Agreements
MGAs operate under agreements that typically define:
- Binding authority (what lines, limits, carriers)
- Underwriting guidelines must be followed
- Reporting and accounting requirements
- E&O insurance requirements
- State licensing requirements for MGA operations

**Key point:** MGA agreements are contracts — regulatory compliance and contractual obligations both apply simultaneously.

### Producer Compensation Disclosure
Several states require disclosure of compensation arrangements to consumers. NY Reg 194 is the most demanding model. Trend toward broader disclosure requirements nationally.

### Independent Agency Aggregator Relationships
When operating through aggregators (First Connect, Smart Choice, etc.):
- Agency is responsible for compliance even when using aggregator technology
- Consumer-facing materials must comply with state-specific requirements
- Appointment status with each carrier matters for licensing compliance

## Digital Distribution Considerations

### E-SIGN Act / UETA
Federal and state laws allowing electronic signatures and records in insurance transactions. Most insurance transactions can be completed electronically, but:
- Consumer must consent to electronic delivery
- Certain documents may still require paper delivery in some states
- Life insurance has additional state-specific electronic delivery rules

### Online Quoting Compliance
- Quotes presented online must be accurate and compliant with state rating rules
- "Bait and switch" pricing is a market conduct violation
- If binding authority exists, online binding triggers all standard policy issuance requirements

### Data Use in Underwriting
Using third-party data sources (credit, public records, IoT, telematics) in underwriting:
- Must be filed and approved where required
- FCRA compliance if using consumer report data
- State insurance regulations may restrict certain data types (some states prohibit credit scoring for certain lines)

## Emerging / Watch Areas

- **State AI bulletin adoption** — NAIC AI bulletin being adopted state by state. Expect 40+ states to have some form within 2 years.
- **Embedded insurance** — new distribution model drawing regulatory attention. Affinity and embedded products face additional disclosure scrutiny.
- **Parametric and usage-based insurance** — new product structures attracting regulatory examination of rating and underwriting rules.
- **Climate risk disclosure** — NAIC and state DOIs increasingly requiring insurer climate risk disclosures.

## Key Obligations Checklist

- [ ] Active license in good standing in each state of operation
- [ ] Carrier appointments current and properly documented
- [ ] E&O coverage maintained at required limits
- [ ] Privacy notice provided to consumers (GLBA/Model 672)
- [ ] Cybersecurity program in place (Model 668 where adopted)
- [ ] Advertisement and marketing materials comply with state rules
- [ ] E-SIGN/UETA consumer consent obtained for electronic transactions
- [ ] AML program if selling life/annuity products
- [ ] Compensation disclosure where required
